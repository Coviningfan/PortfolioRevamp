When a small business decides their team cannot keep up with the phone, they usually call one of two kinds of vendor: a traditional answering service (Ruby, AnswerConnect, Smith.ai, MAP, regional players) or a modern AI receptionist provider. The two look superficially similar — both pick up the phone when you cannot — but the economics, the workflow, and what you get on the other side of the call are nothing alike.

Here is the honest comparison.

## What a traditional answering service actually does

A traditional answering service routes your overflow calls to a human at a remote call center who follows a script you provided. They greet the caller in your business name, collect a few fields, and either forward the call or send you a message.

That is a real and useful product. Doctors' offices, law firms, and HVAC companies have used answering services since long before VoIP existed. They are good at three things: sounding human, handling unusual situations gracefully, and being legally accountable for what they say.

They are not good at four things:

1. **Cost.** Pricing is per connected minute. The going rate in 2026 is roughly **$1.20 to $2.10 per minute** depending on plan tier, with a minimum monthly commitment (often $200–$400) and overage charges that scale fast.
2. **Coverage.** "24/7" usually means "we have a human awake somewhere," which usually means the night shift is in a different country and the script handoff is rougher than the day team.
3. **Data hygiene.** What you get back is a typed message — sometimes an email, sometimes a CRM note via a basic integration. The information is unstructured and the lag from call to CRM can be hours.
4. **Variability.** Different operators handle calls differently. Quality drifts. Your brand voice on the phone is whoever picked up.

## What a modern AI receptionist actually does

A modern AI voice agent — the kind we build at DSX Edge on top of [3CX V20 Update 8](/blog/3cx-v20-update-8-what-it-means-for-business) — answers in your business voice within two rings, qualifies the caller against a workflow built specifically for your business, and writes a structured record (intent, fields captured, recording, transcript) straight into your CRM in real time.

It costs roughly **$0.10 to $0.35 per call** at typical small-business volumes — not per minute, per call. It runs 24/7 without a shift change. It never has a bad day. And it gets better every week because the underlying voice models keep improving without you doing anything.

What it is not yet good at:

- Genuine emotional nuance. A grief call to a funeral home should not be answered by an AI, full stop.
- Complex novel situations. If a caller starts describing a regulatory edge case that is not in the workflow, the AI should escalate, not improvise.
- Negotiation. Pricing escalations, contract disputes, anything where the caller is upset about money — those go to a human.

## The side-by-side that actually matters

Pretend you run a 12-person plumbing company doing 600 inbound calls a month. Roughly 65% of those calls happen during business hours when your dispatcher is on, and 35% are after hours or overflow.

That 210 after-hours-and-overflow calls per month is what you are trying to cover.

**Traditional answering service.** At an average of 3 minutes per connected call and $1.50/minute, you are at roughly **$945/month**, plus the monthly minimum. Calls get typed up and emailed to dispatch. Average lag from call end to CRM update: ~45 minutes. Bookings happen the next morning when dispatch reads the email.

**AI receptionist.** At ~$0.25 per call, you are at roughly **$53/month** in usage. Add a flat implementation cost amortized over 12 months and a platform fee, and most small businesses land around **$300–$500/month all-in**. Calls are qualified, transcribed, and CRM-updated in real time. Bookings are confirmed before the caller hangs up.

For most businesses that math is not close. The AI is dramatically cheaper *and* delivers more value per call. The exceptions are real but narrow — see the next section.

## When you should still pick a human answering service

There are workflows where AI is the wrong tool:

- **Bereavement, hospice, mental health, crisis lines.** Use humans. Period.
- **High-end concierge brands.** If your customer base expects a named, recognized receptionist who knows them personally, AI is not the right experience.
- **Single-shop legal intake for very sensitive matters.** A trained paralegal-grade human is often worth the cost.

For everyone else, the answer is not "either / or." It is **AI in front, human escalation behind**. The AI handles the 80% of calls that are routine — appointment booking, status updates, qualification, FAQs — and explicitly escalates to a human (yours, or a partnered answering service) for the 20% that needs nuance. This is the model we cover in [Three Things to Do Before You Turn On an AI Receptionist](/blog/three-things-to-do-before-ai-receptionist).

## The hidden cost answering services do not advertise

The line-item cost is only half the picture. The hidden cost of a traditional answering service is **call-to-action lag**.

When the answering service emails you a message at 11:14 p.m. about a flooding basement, and your dispatcher does not see the email until 7:30 the next morning, that customer called another plumber at 11:16 p.m. The lead is gone. You paid $4 for the call and lost a $1,800 job.

An AI receptionist that can read your dispatch on-call schedule, identify "flooding basement" as urgent, and SMS your on-call tech directly within 90 seconds is not just cheaper — it captures revenue the answering service was leaking.

## The cleanest mental model

Think of it this way:

- **An answering service replaces a receptionist.**
- **An AI receptionist replaces a workflow.**

The receptionist takes a message. The workflow books the job, updates the CRM, dispatches the right tech, sends the confirmation text, and posts the follow-up reminder for 48 hours later. That is what you are actually paying for, and that is why the per-minute pricing of a traditional answering service stops making sense once you do the math.
